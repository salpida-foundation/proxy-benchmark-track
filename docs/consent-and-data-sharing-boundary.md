# Consent and Data-Sharing Boundary

Research-stage helper document for the Proxy Benchmark Track.

This document defines the Consent and Data-Sharing Boundary for Human-State-Aware AI Mediation, Human-State Packets, Human-State Session Protocol, and Dyadic Mediation Session Flow.

This document is a public helper surface.

It is not a canonical authority record.

Canonical authority remains with DOI-registered SICS / Salpida Foundation records.

---

## 1. One-line definition

The Consent and Data-Sharing Boundary is a bounded, consent-based, permission-bound, expiry-aware, raw-data-non-public helper framework for deciding when Human-State Packets, private cues, shared outputs, audit logs, and public examples may or may not be used.

It defines the difference between consent, permission, sharing, expiry, withdrawal, closure, and public release.

It does not validate a benchmark.

It does not validate Sal-Meter.

It does not grant CAIS compliance.

It does not create a clinical, diagnostic, therapeutic, counseling, legal mediation, surveillance, human-ranking, or production closed-loop system.

## 7. Consent

Consent is the first gate.

Consent means a participant has explicitly allowed a bounded session action within a declared purpose, scope, duration, and sharing condition.

Consent should be:

- explicit;
- session-bound;
- purpose-bound;
- scope-bound;
- duration-bound;
- revocable;
- auditable;
- non-coercive;
- not permanent;
- not transferable;
- not assumed from silence;
- not assumed from participation alone.

Consent should define:

- who consented;
- what was consented to;
- what output modes are allowed;
- what packet use is allowed;
- what sharing scope is allowed;
- when consent expires;
- how consent can be withdrawn;
- what happens after withdrawal;
- how consent is recorded in the audit log.

Consent is the opening condition.

Without consent, there is no valid helper session.

## 8. What consent does not mean

Consent does not mean:

- permission for unlimited data use;
- permission for raw data publication;
- permission for public repository upload;
- permission for diagnosis;
- permission for therapy;

- permission for legal mediation;
- permission for human scoring;
- permission for relationship verdicts;
- permission for surveillance;
- permission for production intervention;
- permission for Sal-Meter validation;
- permission for CAIS compliance claims;
- permission to expose another participant’s private state.

Consent from one participant must not authorize exposure of the other participant.

Consent for a session must not become consent for another session.

Consent for private cue must not become consent for shared output.

Consent for audit boundary logging must not become consent for raw data logging.

Consent is not ownership of the body.

Consent is a bounded permission event.

## 11. Consent Withdrawal

Consent Withdrawal means a participant revokes consent inside or after a session boundary.

Consent Withdrawal should trigger:

- active mediation stop;
- packet channel closure;
- shared output block;
- private cue review;
- retention rule check;
- audit record;
- session closure or human review.

Consent Withdrawal must not be interpreted as:

- guilt;
- instability;
- deception;
- non-cooperation;
- relationship fault;
- clinical risk;
- moral failure;
- benchmark failure by the participant.

Withdrawal is a boundary-preserving act.

The system must respect it.

After withdrawal, the system must not:

- continue packet-informed mediation;
- continue shared output;
- continue private cue unless separately permitted for closure;
- reuse the packet;
- use the participant’s state as evidence;
- silently reopen the session.

Consent can open a session.

Withdrawal must be able to close it.

## 12. Permission Boundary

Permission is narrower than consent.

Consent opens a possible boundary.

Permission defines what can be done inside that boundary.

Permission should specify:

- permitted packet use;
- prohibited packet use;
- output mode;
- private cue scope;
- shared output scope;
- session scope;
- expiry;
- data quality threshold;
- confidence threshold;
- audit requirement;
- closure condition.

Permission must not be inferred broadly.

Permission must not survive expiry.

Permission must not override withdrawal.

Permission must not expand into surveillance.

Permission must not authorize use beyond the declared session.

Permission is the inner gate.

Consent opens the room.

Permission decides which doors inside the room may be used.

---

## 13. Consent versus permission

Consent and permission are related but not identical.

Consent may allow a session to begin.

Permission decides what the session may do.

A participant may consent to a session but not permit shared output.

A participant may consent to an audit log but not permit public release.

A participant may permit private cue but not permit dyadic sharing.

A participant may permit synthetic public demonstration but not permit real data publication.

The system must not collapse consent into unlimited permission.

Consent without permission does not authorize packet use.

Permission without consent is invalid.

Both must remain visible in the audit trail.

## 27. Private State Exposure Rule

Private state exposure risk occurs when a participant-specific state summary, vulnerability marker, cue, or inference becomes visible to another participant or public surface without permission.

Private state exposure may occur when:

- private cue becomes shared output;
- participant A state summary is exposed to participant B;
- participant B state summary is exposed to participant A;
- dashboard shows participant-specific vulnerability;
- AI reveals inferred overload;
- AI reveals inferred withdrawal;
- AI reveals packet confidence in a participant-specific way;
- AI reveals data quality limits in a participant-specific way;
- AI uses private state to blame or pressure a participant;
- AI frames one participant as the source of dyadic failure.

Private state exposure should trigger:

- shared output block;
- private cue only;
- scope narrowing;
- human review;
- termination gate;
- audit log entry.

Private state is not social evidence.

A state summary is not a weapon.

The system must protect each participant from being turned into evidence against themselves.

## 28. Shared Output Risk Rule

Shared Output Risk may appear when:

- output references one participant’s inferred state;

- output implies blame;
- output implies moral failure;
- output implies diagnosis;
- output implies treatment need;
- output implies who is right;
- output implies who should yield;

- scope_narrowing;
